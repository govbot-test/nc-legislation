Section 1:
GENERAL ASSEMBLY OF NORTH CAROLINA
SESSION 2025
H 1
Short Title: DPI to Redesign Math Instruction. (Public)
Sponsors: Representatives Willis, Biggs, Pickett, and Rhyne (Primary Sponsors).
For a complete list of sponsors, refer to the North Carolina General Assembly web site.
Referred to: Education - K-12, if favorable, State and Local Government, if favorable, Rules,
Calendar, and Operations of the House
April 10, 2025
1 A BILL TO BE ENTITLED
2 AN ACT TO DIRECT THE DEPARTMENT OF PUBLIC INSTRUCTION TO PROVIDE
3 CERTAIN MATHEMATICS SUPPORTS IN SCHOOLS AND TO REPORT TO THE
4 GENERAL ASSEMBLY ON THOSE SUPPORTS.
5 The General Assembly of North Carolina enacts:
6 SECTION 1.(a) Article 8 of Chapter 115C of the General Statutes is amended by
7 adding a new Part to read:
8 "Part 1C. Mathematics Instruction.
9 "§ 115C-83.20. North Carolina mathematics.
10 The General Assembly wants all students to become proficient in grade-level math as defined
11 by State standards in order to prepare to take and pass NC Math 1 and the related EOC and ensure
12 they are college and career ready. To attain these goals, all students should have access to
13 high-quality systems of math instruction that include math facts, procedural fluency, conceptual
14 learning, and problem solving. These systems should be based on the best evidence on how
15 students learn math and include core instructional materials and professional development.
16 "§ 115C-83.22. Definitions.
17 The following definitions apply in this Part:
18 (1) Explicit math instruction. – A form of high-quality instruction in which, for
19 some portion of a lesson or intervention, the teacher provides clear modeling,
20 think-alouds, worked examples, practice, and timely corrective feedback
21 directly to students.
22 (2) High-quality math instructional materials. – Strategies and materials, aligned
23 to State standards, where students are guided through a coherent progression
24 of topics, skills, and approaches to learning math which include a mix of
25 explicit instruction, practice, conceptual reasoning, and problem solving.
26 These strategies and materials simultaneously develop students' conceptual
27 understanding, procedural fluency, ability to apply concepts, and
28 problem-solving skills.
29 (3) Mathematical deficiency. – When a student does not meet the minimum State
30 standard math skills for their grade level. These skills include at least:
31 one-to-one correspondence, cardinality, number sense, counting, and the four
32 basic operations.
33 (4) Mathematical discourse. – Opportunities for students to reason
34 mathematically and discuss with their peers and teacher how they are thinking

1 about mathematics, including use of math-specific vocabulary, procedural
2 steps, concepts, and problem-solving strategies.
3 (5) Mathematical proficiency. – A demonstrated understanding of a mathematical
4 standard, which serves as foundational prior knowledge on which to build new
5 learning.
6 (6) Responsive feedback. – Immediate, interactive feedback made possible by
7 technology that shows students the mathematical meaning of their thinking
8 and guides them to develop stronger conceptual understanding, procedural
9 fluency, ability to apply concepts, and problem-solving skills.
10 (7) Structured approach to problem-based learning. – Strategies and materials that
11 develop students' curiosity into lasting grade-level understanding using
12 structured lessons and engaging tasks. This approach uses step-by-step
13 instruction to systematically build on students' prior knowledge by combining
14 conceptual understanding, procedural fluency, and application in a
15 pedagogically coherent sequence.
16 "§ 115C-83.24. Department of Public Instruction mathematics supports.
17 The Department shall provide a system of support for all students in kindergarten through
18 grade eight and teachers of students in those grades based on high-quality math instructional
19 materials and utilizing a structured approach to problem-based learning. The system shall, where
20 appropriate and reasonable, leverage technology to engage students and provide them with
21 responsive feedback, while also providing teachers with actionable, instructional insights. The
22 system of support shall include the following:
23 (1) An approved list of one or more reliable, valid, high-quality supplemental
24 math systems that districts shall choose from to use for screening and progress
25 monitoring toward grade-level math. The supplemental math system shall
26 meet all of the following:
27 a. Measure a number of age-appropriate skills that include, but are not
28 limited to, one-to-one correspondence, cardinality, number sense,
29 counting, the four basic operations, addition and multiplication facts,
30 measurement, fractions, and geometry.
31 b. Identify students who have a math deficiency, including identifying
32 students with characteristics of dyscalculia.
33 c. Identify the areas of mathematical proficiency that each student has so
34 teachers can build on what students already understand to inform
35 differentiated instruction and appropriate interventions.
36 d. Include a system of parent or guardian notifications that will describe,
37 in understandable language to the parent or guardian, the nature of a
38 student's math deficiency and areas of proficiency on which to build
39 no later than 15 calendar days after the identification of this deficiency.
40 The notification will include the current services being provided to the
41 student, proposed interventions and materials to address the
42 deficiency, and strategies for parents or guardians to use at home.
43 (2) An approved list of one or more high-quality instructional materials that
44 utilize a structured approach to problem-based learning that districts must
45 choose from to be used for math support. Each student in kindergarten through
46 grade eight shall be provided an appropriate math intervention to address their
47 specific needs. The math intervention program will do the following:
48 a. Provide explicit instruction that is systematic and sequentially aligned
49 to grade-level standards and builds on areas of mathematical
50 proficiency.

1 b. Provide targeted and flexible small group or individualized math
2 interventions based on student need.
3 c. Allow for consistent and clear practice and reinforcement of critical
4 math concepts to ensure all students reach grade-level proficiency.
5 (3) An approved list of one or more high-quality professional learning offerings
6 that districts must choose from the following for teachers in kindergarten
7 through grade eight:
8 a. Comprehensive training on high-quality math instruction and using a
9 structured approach to problem-based learning. Additionally, where
10 appropriate and reasonable, how to leverage technology in the
11 mathematics classroom.
12 b. The Department approved supplemental math systems selected by
13 school districts to ensure teachers have the knowledge and skills to
14 administer the supplemental math systems and use the resulting data
15 to inform instruction based on student need.
16 (4) An approved list of one or more high-quality math instructional materials that
17 utilize a structured approach to problem-based learning, including both core
18 curriculum and supplemental materials, that districts must choose for students
19 in kindergarten through grade eight. These materials shall meet all of the
20 following criteria:
21 a. Be based on the best evidence of how students learn to do math and
22 utilize structured problem-based learning.
23 b. Include responsive feedback for students as well as numerous
24 opportunities for student-to-student mathematical discourse.
25 c. Have an appropriate pedagogical and developmental balance of print
26 and digital content.
27 d. Builds on students' areas of mathematical proficiency and prior
28 knowledge to develop new learning.
29 e. Align with current State math content standards."
30 SECTION 1.(b) The Department of Public Instruction shall report to the Joint
31 Legislative Education Oversight Committee prior to the implementation of the first mathematics
32 standards adopted by the State Board of Education after the effective date of this act. The report
33 shall include the following:
34 (1) A summary of the mathematics standards adopted by the State Board.
35 (2) Mathematics supports to be provided by the Department pursuant to
36 G.S. 115C-83.24, as enacted by this act.
37 (3) Any other information the Department deems relevant.
38 SECTION 2. This act is effective when it becomes law and applies beginning with
39 the 2025-2026 school year.
