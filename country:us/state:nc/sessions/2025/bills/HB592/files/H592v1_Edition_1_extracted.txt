Section 1:
GENERAL ASSEMBLY OF NORTH CAROLINA
SESSION 2025
H 1
Short Title: Toxic-Free Medical Devices Act of 2025. (Public)
Sponsors: Representatives Reeder and Rhyne (Primary Sponsors).
For a complete list of sponsors, refer to the North Carolina General Assembly web site.
Referred to: Health, if favorable, Rules, Calendar, and Operations of the House
April 1, 2025
1 A BILL TO BE ENTITLED
2 AN ACT TO PROHIBIT THE MANUFACTURING, SELLING, AND DISTRIBUTING OF
3 INTRAVENOUS SOLUTION CONTAINERS AND INTRAVENOUS TUBING THAT
4 ARE INTENTIONALLY MADE WITH DEHP.
5 The General Assembly of North Carolina enacts:
6 SECTION 1. Chapter 130A of the General Statutes is amended by adding a new
7 Article to read as follows:
8 "Article 19C.
9 "DEHP Hazard Management.
10 "§ 130A-453.33. Legislative finding.
11 The General Assembly finds all of the following:
12 (1) DEHP and other ortho-phthalates are toxic chemicals used primarily to
13 produce flexibility in plastics, mainly polyvinyl chloride (PVC).
14 (2) DEHP is the most common plasticizer used in medical devices, including
15 intravenous solution containers, which are also known as IV bags, and
16 intravenous tubing.
17 (3) Over the course of its shelf life, DEHP leaches from IV bags and tubing made
18 from DEHP into the solutions being held in the medical devices.
19 (4) DEHP is classified by the United States Environmental Protection Agency as
20 an endocrine-disrupting compound since it can:
21 a. Interfere with the hormonal system in humans and animals.
22 b. Mimic or block the actions of hormones, leading to adverse effects on
23 reproductive health, development, and metabolism.
24 (5) DEHP exposure has been associated with adverse effects on reproductive
25 organs and fertility. DEHP can also disrupt normal reproductive development,
26 reduce sperm quality, and affect hormone levels in both males and females.
27 (6) DEHP is metabolized in the liver and can accumulate in the body over time.
28 Prolonged exposure to high levels of DEHP has been shown to cause liver and
29 kidney damage in animal studies.
30 (7) Inhalation or ingestion of DEHP can cause respiratory irritation and allergic
31 reactions in some individuals, particularly those with preexisting respiratory
32 conditions or sensitivities.
33 (8) Studies have suggested a potential link between DEHP exposure and certain
34 types of cancer, including breast, liver, lung, and testicular cancer.

1 (9) The United States Environmental Protection Agency has determined that
2 DEHP is a probable human carcinogen.
3 (10) The leaching of DEHP from medical devices at varying concentrations has
4 been linked to multidrug resistance in breast cancer cells, inhibiting the
5 effectiveness of breast cancer drugs. This phenomenon has been observed at
6 both high and low concentrations of DEHP, highlighting the potential impact
7 of DEHP leaching on cancer treatment outcomes.
8 (11) Exposure to DEHP has been linked to multidrug resistance in triple-negative
9 breast cancer cells, inhibiting the apoptosis mechanism induced by breast
10 cancer drugs, such as tamoxifen, and increasing cell proliferation.
11 (12) DEHP has been suggested to serve as a mitogenic factor for estrogen
12 receptor-positive breast cancer cells, potentially making them multidrug
13 resistant.
14 "§ 130A-453.34. Definitions.
15 The following definitions apply in this Article:
16 (1) DEHP. – Di(2-ethylhexyl) phthalate.
17 (2) Health care practitioner. – An individual who is authorized to practice some
18 component of the healing arts by a license, permit, certificate, or registration
19 issued by a State licensing agency or board.
20 (3) Intentionally added DEHP. – Any of the following:
21 a. DEHP that a manufacturer has intentionally added to a product and
22 that has a functional or technical effect on the product.
23 b. DEHP that is an intentional breakdown product of an added chemical.
24 (4) Intravenous solution container. – A container used to house medicine, fluid,
25 or nutrition therapy that is intravenously delivered to a patient in a hospital,
26 outpatient facility, or other health care facility.
27 (5) Intravenous tubing. – Tubing used to intravenously administer fluids,
28 medication, or nutrients directly to an adult, child, or infant.
29 (6) Ortho-phthalate. – A class of chemicals that are esters of ortho-phthalic acid,
30 including DEHP or any of the following:
31 a. Benzyl butyl phthalate (BBP).
32 b. Dibutyl phthalate (DBP).
33 c. Dicyclohexyl phthalate (DCHP).
34 d. Diethyl phthalate (DEP).
35 e. Diisobutyl phthalate (DIBP).
36 f. Diisodecyl phthalate (DIDP).
37 g. Diisononyl phthalate (DINP).
38 h. Di-n-hexyl phthalate (DnHP).
39 i. Di-n-octyl phthalate (DNOP).
40 j. Di-n-pentyl phthalate (DnPP).
41 k. Diisoheptyl phthalate (DIHP).
42 "§ 130A-453.35. Prohibitions.
43 (a) Intravenous Solution Containers. – Beginning January 1, 2030, a person or entity shall
44 not manufacture, sell, or distribute into commerce in the State of North Carolina intravenous
45 solution containers made with intentionally added DEHP.
46 (b) Intravenous Tubing. – Beginning January 1, 2035, a person or entity shall not
47 manufacture, sell, or distribute into commerce in the State of North Carolina intravenous tubing
48 made with intentionally added DEHP.
49 (c) Replacement. – A person may not replace DEHP, pursuant to this Article, with
50 another ortho-phthalate in a new or revised medical device.

General Assembly Of North Carolina Session 2025
1 (d) Maximum Quantity. – An intravenous solution container or intravenous tubing
2 product shall not have unintentionally added DEHP present at a quantity at or above 0.1 percent
3 weight per weight (w/w).
4 (e) Exemptions. – The following items, as described in Title 21 of the Code of Federal
5 Regulations, are exempt from these provisions:
6 (1) Human blood collection and storage bags.
7 (2) Apheresis and cell therapy blood kits and bags, including integral tubing."
8 SECTION 2. G.S. 130A-22(b3) reads as rewritten:
9 "(b3) The Secretary may impose an administrative penalty on a person who violates Article
10 19A or 19B Article 19A, 19B, or 19C of this Chapter or any rules adopted pursuant to Article
11 19A or 19B Article 19A, 19B, or 19C of this Chapter. Each day of a continuing violation is a
12 separate violation. The penalty shall not exceed five thousand dollars ($5,000) for each day the
13 violation continues for Article 19A of this Chapter. The penalty shall not exceed five thousand
14 dollars ($5,000) for each day the violation continues for Article 19B of this Chapter. The penalty
15 shall not exceed five thousand dollars ($5,000) for each day the violation continues for Article
16 19C of this Chapter. The penalty authorized by this section does not apply to a person who is not
17 required to be certified under Article 19A or 19B."
18 SECTION 3. Except as otherwise provided, this act is effective when it becomes
19 law.
