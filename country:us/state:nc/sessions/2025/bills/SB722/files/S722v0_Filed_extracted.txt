8 SECTION 1.(a) Chapter 114 of the General Statutes is amended by adding a new
9 Article to read:
10 "Article 11.
11 "Online Safety Division.
12 "§ 114-75. Division established; duties.
13 (a) There is hereby established in the Office of the Attorney General of North Carolina,
14 the Online Safety Division (Division). The attorneys assigned to this Division shall function as
15 an independent regulatory office within the Department of Justice focusing on online safety, with
16 an emphasis on the protection of children online. The Division shall have the following duties:
17 (1) Investigation of complaints made under the Chapter 114B of the General
18 Statute (Children's Online Safety Act).
19 (2) Educating of law enforcement agencies and the general public about the online
20 safety of all North Carolinians, with an emphasis targeting harmful activities
21 and dark patterns.
22 (3) Issuance of online safety standards and guidelines, and review of relevant
23 industry codes pertaining to internet safety, age-appropriate design, and
24 compliance with the Children's Online Safety Act.
25 (4) Facilitating advisory panels on internet safety, including child development
26 experts, technology specialists, parent representatives, community
27 stakeholders, and industry representatives."
28 SECTION 1.(b) Effective July 1, 2025, there is appropriated from the General Fund
29 to the Department of Justice the sum of five million dollars ($5,000,000) for the 2025-2026 fiscal
30 year and the sum of four million five hundred thousand dollars ($4,500,000) for the 2026-2027
31 fiscal year to establish the Child Safety Division as enacted by this section.

16 "§ 114B-2. Duty of care established; mandated protections for children; requirements for
17 covered platforms.
18 (a) It is the public policy of this State that its children are owed a duty of care with regard
19 to their online activities in order to limit foreseeable harm and their exposure to dark patterns and
20 harmful content on covered platforms. Further, the General Assembly endorses age-appropriate
21 design and strong parental controls as central to protecting children in online service
22 environments.
23 (b) Covered platforms shall require parental notification for accounts created by children
24 and must offer robust, easy-to-use parental supervision tools such as filtering options, contact
25 management, time limits and scheduling, purchase controls, and activity reporting.
26 (c) It is unlawful for covered platforms to use dark patterns or deploy features known to
27 be addictive or manipulative.
28 (d) Covered platforms shall establish clear definitions of what constitutes cyberbullying
29 and include provisions for direct reporting to the Division, as well as provide intervention and
30 support services for affected children, including mediation options where appropriate.
31 (e) Each covered platform shall submit to the Online Safety Division an annual Child
32 Impact Assessment for new an existing services on the platform. The assessment shall include
33 documentation of potential risks to children and assessment of addiction and compulsive usage
34 risks. The documentation supporting each annual assessment shall be retained for at least a
35 three-year period
36 (f) Covered platforms shall utilize the highest privacy settings by default for all users
37 reasonably likely to be children and shall establish strict data minimization principles to include
38 the following:
39 (1) Limiting collection of personal data to what is necessary for the service.
40 (2) Requiring deletion when no longer needed.
41 (3) Prohibiting data use for commercial purposes unless strictly necessary.
42 (4) Including "Right to be Forgotten" provisions empowering minors to request
43 deletion of their data and content.
44 (5) Prohibiting profiling and behavioral advertising targeting children.
45 (6) Requiring child-friendly privacy information and controls.
46 (7) Mandating transparency about personal data use.
47 (8) Establishing clear restrictions on geolocation data collection and use.
48 (9) Data broker restrictions for children's information.

33 SECTION 4.(a) There is hereby established the North Carolina Online Child Safety
34 Commission as an independent regulatory body with oversight authority on matters relating to
35 the safety and wellbeing of minors in digital environments. The Commission's primary mission
36 shall be to protect North Carolina's children from online harms through research, education,
37 regulation, enforcement, and ongoing adaptation to the evolving digital landscape. The
38 Commission shall serve as the state's foremost authority on online child safety, advancing the
39 digital wellbeing of minors while respecting fundamental rights. The Commission shall be guided
40 by the best interests of the child, the recognition that digital safety is fundamental to childhood
41 development, the understanding that technology must adapt to children's needs rather than the
42 reverse, and the principle that powerful digital platforms must be held accountable for the safety
43 of young users. The Commission shall consist of nine (9) members with demonstrated expertise
44 and commitment to child welfare, digital technology, mental health, education, or related fields
45 relevant to children's online safety. Members shall be appointed as follows:
46 (1) Three (5) members appointed by the Governor
47 (2) Two (1) members appointed by the President Pro Tempore of the Senate
48 (3) Two (1) members appointed by the Speaker of the House of Representatives
49 (4) One (1) member appointed by the Attorney General
50 (5) One (1) member appointed by the Superintendent of Public Instruction.
51 The Commission membership shall include, at minimum:

13 Department of Justice may do all of the following:
14 (1) Issue binding regulations implementing the provisions of this act.
15 (2) Establish safety standards for covered platforms.
16 (3) Review and approve industry codes of practice.
17 (4) Initiate investigations into potential violations.
18 (5) Issue orders requiring compliance with the act.
19 (6) Impose penalties for violations as provided in this act.
20 (7) Seek injunctive relief through the courts when necessary to prevent harm to
21 children.
22 (b) The Commission shall provide expert guidance to:
23 (1) The Governor and General Assembly on matters relating to online child
24 safety.
25 (2) State agencies on implementation of digital safety programs.
26 (3) Educational institutions on digital literacy and safety curricula.
27 (4) Parents and caregivers on tools and strategies to protect children online.
28 (5) Technology platforms on best practices for age-appropriate design.
29 (c) Research and data collection. – The Commission shall:
30 (1) Conduct or commission research on emerging online risks to children.
31 (2) Collect and analyze data on the prevalence and impact of online harms.
32 (3) Monitor global developments in online safety regulation.
33 (4) Evaluate the effectiveness of interventions and regulations.
34 (5) Maintain a database of safety incidents, platform responses, and outcomes.
35 (d) Educational Initiatives. – The Commission shall develop and oversee:
36 (1) Public awareness campaigns on online child safety.
37 (2) Resource development for schools, parents, and children.
38 (3) Training programs for educators and other professionals.
39 (4) Digital literacy standards for K-12 education.
40 (e) Complaint handling. – The Commission shall:
41 (1) Establish accessible mechanisms for receiving complaints about online harms
42 to children.
43 (2) Create specialized response protocols for different categories of harm.
44 (3) Oversee platform compliance with complaint response requirements.
45 (4) Intervene directly in serious cases where platforms fail to act appropriately.
46 (5) Provide support resources for affected children and families.
47 (f) Coordination and collaboration. – The Commission shall:
48 (1) Coordinate with relevant state agencies including the Department of Justice,
49 Department of Health and Human Services, and Department of Public
50 Instruction.

7 SECTION 4.(c) Annual duties and reporting requirements. – Key language
8 elements:
9 (a) State of Children's Online Safety Assessment. – The Commission shall prepare and
10 publish an annual "State of Children's Online Safety in North Carolina" report that:
11 (1) Assesses current online risks facing North Carolina's children.
12 (2) Analyzes trends in online harms and platform responses.
13 (3) Evaluates platform compliance with this act and related regulations.
14 (4) Identifies emerging threats and technologies of concern.
15 (5) Measures progress in addressing previously identified issues.
16 (6) Provides data-driven insights on the digital experiences of children by age

18 (b) Platform Compliance Review. – The Commission shall conduct annual compliance
19 reviews of covered platforms that:
20 (1) Evaluate implementation of required safety measures.
21 (2) Assess the effectiveness of age verification systems.
22 (3) Review Child Impact Assessments and safety documentation.
23 (4) Analyze complaint handling and response times.
24 (5) Examine algorithmic systems for compliance with safety standards.
25 (6) Identify best practices and areas requiring improvement.
26 (7) Result in public compliance ratings for each major platform.
27 (c) Legislative Recommendations. – The Commission shall submit annual
28 recommendations to the General Assembly that:
29 (1) Identify necessary amendments to this act based on technological
30 developments.
31 (2) Propose new legislative measures to address emerging concerns.
32 (3) Suggest improvements to enforcement mechanisms.
33 (4) Recommend funding priorities for child online safety initiatives.
34 (5) Identify areas where federal action or coordination is needed.
35 (d) Public Hearings and Testimony. – The Commission shall:
36 (1) Hold at least four public hearings annually across different regions of the
37 State.
38 (2) Receive testimony from children, parents, educators, platforms, and experts.
39 (3) Conduct specialized hearings on emerging issues of concern.
40 (4) Ensure diverse perspectives are represented in deliberations.
41 (5) Make hearing records publicly available.
42 (e) Industry Engagement. – The Commission shall:
43 (1) Convene an annual Industry Safety Summit with platform representatives.
44 (2) Facilitate regular working groups on specific safety challenges.
45 (3) Review and approve updates to industry codes of practice.
46 (4) Evaluate voluntary safety initiatives.
47 (5) Promote adoption of safety innovations across the industry.
48 (f) Transparency Reporting. – The Commission shall publish annual transparency reports
49 detailing:
50 (1) Enforcement actions taken and their outcomes.
51 (2) Complaints received and resolved.
