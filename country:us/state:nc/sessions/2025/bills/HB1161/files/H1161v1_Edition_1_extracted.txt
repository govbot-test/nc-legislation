14 "§ 163-278.18A. Prohibit use of artificial intelligence in political advertisements.
15 (a) For purposes of this section, the following definitions shall apply:
16 (1) Artificial intelligence. – The capability of computer systems or algorithms to
17 imitate intelligent human behavior. The term includes generative artificial
18 intelligence.
19 (2) Political advertisement. – An advertisement as defined under
20 G.S. 163-278.38Z(1), including communications sent by email, text, or
21 appearing on a website or social media platforms.
22 (b) No candidate, candidate campaign committee, political party organization, political
23 action committee, referendum committee, individual, or other sponsor shall use artificial
24 intelligence in any form of political advertisement, including, but not limited to, images, videos,
25 voices, or writings.

27 1 misdemeanor and pay a fine to the State Board based on the cost to produce and distribute the
28 artificial intelligence-generated political advertisement multiplied by 10. In addition, the
29 following shall apply:
30 (1) If a candidate's campaign committee is convicted for a violation under this
31 section, the campaign committee is prohibited from soliciting donations or
32 making contributions for two years from the date of conviction, regardless of
33 whether an election is occurring.
34 (2) If a political action committee is convicted for a violation under this section,
35 the political action committee shall have its bank account frozen and shall not

26 PART III. EMPLOYMENT
27 SECTION 3.1.(a) Effective October 1, 2026, the General Statutes are amended by

29 "Chapter 95A.
30 "Fair Artificial Intelligence Hiring Act.
31 "§ 95A-1. Title; findings.
32 (a) This Chapter shall be known and may be cited as the "North Carolina Fair AI Hiring
33 Act (NC-FAHA)."
34 (b) The General Assembly finds that:
35 (1) Artificial intelligence and algorithmic decision-making tools are increasingly
36 used by employers in North Carolina to screen, rank, evaluate, and select
37 applicants for employment and to evaluate employees for promotion.
38 However, these AI tools can perpetuate or amplify patterns of discrimination
39 based on race, sex, ethnicity, national origin, disability, age, and other
40 protected characteristics, often through mechanisms that are opaque to both
41 applicants and employers.
42 (2) North Carolinians have a substantial interest in ensuring that the use of
43 algorithmic tools in a covered employment decision is subject to independent
44 scrutiny, public accountability, and meaningful notice to employees.
45 (3) Existing anti-discrimination laws prohibit discriminatory employment
46 practices but do not specifically address the disclosure, audit, and notice
47 requirements necessary to effectively regulate automated decision-making
48 tools.
49 (4) It is the public policy of this State that hiring and promotion employment
50 decisions made using artificial intelligence tools must be fair and not
51 perpetuate or amplify patterns of discrimination.

1 "§ 95A-2. Definitions.
2 The following definitions apply in this Chapter:
3 (1) Automated employment decision tool or AEDT. – Any computational
4 process, or any technology that incorporates such a process, derived in whole
5 or in material part from machine learning, statistical modeling, data analytics,
6 or artificial intelligence, that issues a simplified output, including a score,
7 classification, ranking, or recommendation, that is used to substantially assist
8 or replace the exercise of discretionary judgment by an employer or
9 employment agency in making a covered employment decision. A tool does
10 not qualify as an AEDT solely because it translates or transcribes text,
11 performs arithmetic computation on manually entered data without
12 autonomous parameter adjustment, or conducts background checks governed
13 exclusively by the federal Fair Credit Reporting Act, 15 U.S.C. § 1681, et seq.
14 (2) Bias audit. – An impartial evaluation by an independent auditor that at a
15 minimum: (i) calculates the selection rate and scoring rate for each race and
16 ethnicity category, each sex category, and each intersectional race-and-sex
17 category assessed by the AEDT; (ii) calculates the impact ratio for each such
18 category relative to the most-selected or highest-scoring category; (iii)
19 identifies the source and scope of the historical or test data used in the audit;
20 and (iv) states whether the AEDT was found to have a statistically significant
21 adverse impact on any protected category.
22 (3) Covered employment decision. – A decision to hire an individual for, or to
23 promote an employee to, a position within this State, including a remote
24 position performed primarily by an individual who resides within this State.
25 (4) Deployer. – Any employer or employment agency that uses an AEDT to make
26 or substantially influence a covered employment decision, regardless of
27 whether the AEDT was developed by the deployer or by a third party.
28 (5) Employer. – A public or private employer.
29 (6) Employment agency. – Any person that regularly undertakes, with or without
30 compensation, to procure employees for an employer or to procure for
31 employees opportunities to work for an employer.
32 (7) Independent auditor. – A person that: (i) is not employed by and has no
33 financial interest in the deployer or any vendor of the AEDT being audited,
34 other than compensation for the audit itself; (ii) has demonstrated expertise in
35 the statistical or computational methods necessary to evaluate the AEDT; and
36 (iii) conducts the audit in accordance with the standards established by the
37 State Human Resources Commission for State employment or the
38 Commissioner of Labor as to private employers.
39 (8) Private employer. – As defined in G.S. 95-25.2, except that the term does not
40 include public employers.
41 (9) Public employer. – Any State agency, department, or institution; any
42 constituent institution of The University of North Carolina; the North Carolina
43 Community College System; and any local governmental employer that
44 receives State appropriations used in whole or in part to compensate
45 employees or that has any local employees subject to provisions of Chapter
46 126 of the General Statutes.
47 (10) Substantially assist or replace. – Where the output of an AEDT: (i) serves as
48 the sole basis for a covered employment decision; (ii) serves as a weighted
49 factor that overrides or supersedes other evaluation criteria; or (iii) is a
50 determinative threshold below or above which an individual is screened out
51 of further consideration without independent human review. A tool does not

5 "§ 95A-3. Bias audit requirement.
6 (a) No deployer shall use an AEDT to make a covered employment decision in this State
7 unless all of the following conditions are met:
8 (1) The AEDT has been subjected to a bias audit conducted by an independent
9 auditor within the 12-month period immediately preceding each use of the
10 AEDT.
11 (2) A summary of the results of the most recent bias audit is publicly accessible
12 as required by this Chapter.
13 (3) Advance notice of the AEDT's use has been provided as required by this
14 Chapter.
15 (b) A deployer may commission a bias audit of its own AEDT or may use a bias audit
16 commissioned by the vendor that developed or supplied the AEDT, provided that the auditor
17 meets the independence requirements of this Chapter and the audit was conducted within the
18 applicable 12-month period.
19 (c) Where the AEDT is used for the first time and no historical data from the deployer's
20 own use is available, the bias audit may be conducted using test data or historical data from
21 comparable deployers, provided that the audit summary discloses this limitation.
22 (d) The absence of statistically significant adverse impact findings in a bias audit does
23 not constitute a defense to a claim of employment discrimination under any other provision of
24 State or federal law.
25 "§ 95A-4. Disclosure requirement.
26 (a) Within 30 days of completing a bias audit required under G.S. 95A-3, and prior to
27 any use of the AEDT in a covered employment decision, a deployer shall publish on the
28 employment section of its publicly accessible website a summary of the bias audit results that
29 includes all of the following:
30 (1) The date of the bias audit and the name of the independent auditor.
31 (2) The AEDT's name or description and the date the deployer began using it.
32 (3) The source and scope of the data used in the audit, including whether historical
33 or test data was used and any limitations on the data.
34 (4) The selection and scoring rates and impact ratios for each required
35 demographic category.
36 (5) The auditor's findings regarding statistically significant adverse impact, if any.
37 (b) A deployer that does not maintain a public website shall make the required disclosure
38 available upon written request within five business days.
39 (c) Bias audit summaries shall remain publicly accessible for a minimum of three years
40 from the date of publication.
41 "§ 95A-5. Advance notice requirement.
42 (a) No later than 10 business days before using an AEDT to make a covered employment
43 decision with respect to a particular applicant or employee, a deployer shall provide written
44 notice to that individual indicating all of the following information:
45 (1) That an AEDT will be used in connection with the covered employment
46 decision.
47 (2) The qualifications, characteristics, or criteria that the AEDT is designed to
48 evaluate.
49 (3) A hyperlink (URL) to the bias audit summary required under G.S. 95-4 or, if
50 no website exists, instructions for requesting the summary.

15 PART V. COURTS
16 SECTION 5.1.(a) Chapter 7A of the General Statutes is amended by adding a new
17 Article to read:
18 "Article 64.
19 "Use of Artificial Intelligence.
20 "§ 7A-810. Definitions.
21 The following definitions apply in this Article:
22 (1) Artificial intelligence. – A machine-based system that can, for a given set of
23 human-defined objectives, make predictions, recommendations, or decisions
24 influencing real or virtual environments.
25 (2) Deepfake. – A type of media that uses a generative artificial intelligence
26 system to create or alter images, audio, or video in highly realistic ways,
27 including to falsely depict people making statements or completing actions.
28 This definition includes a person using the person's own likeness, image, or
29 voice in such media.
30 (3) Generative artificial intelligence. – Artificial intelligence technology that is
31 capable of creating content such as text, audio, image, or video based on
32 patterns learned from large volumes of data rather than being explicitly
33 programmed with rules. This definition does not include any form of artificial
34 intelligence used only for spell-check, grammar, or editing purposes.
35 (4) Hallucination. – Output from a generative artificial intelligence system that
36 appears plausible but is inaccurate, fabricated, or unsupported by the
37 underlying data or sources, including all of the following:
38 a. Invented statutes, regulations, cases, citations, quotes, or facts.
39 b. Misstatements of existing statutes, regulations, or case holdings.
40 c. Prerecorded videos or statements or live playback portraying a
41 nonexistent person or factual scenario.
42 "§ 7A-811. Judicial power to dismiss.
43 If any party to a case in a trial court uses any form of generative artificial intelligence in a
44 court filing or appearance, regardless of whether a deepfake or hallucination is present, the trial
45 judge may dismiss the case without prejudice.
46 "§ 7A-812. Right to bring a new action or appeal; judicial discretion.
47 The party against whom a case has been dismissed pursuant to G.S. 7A-811 may take either
48 of the following actions:
49 (1) Bring a new action based on the same claim in the same trial court, after the
50 party removes any and all forms of generative artificial intelligence from the
51 court filings and subject to the approval of the trial judge.
